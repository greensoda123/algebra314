title: "Why the Monty Hall Problem Still Feels Wrong"
date: 2026-03-20
---

The Monty Hall problem is one of those questions that feels like it should become 50/50 after a door is opened, but it does not.

You choose 1 of 3 doors.

- Behind 1 door is a car
- Behind 2 doors are goats
- The host knows where the car is
- The host always opens a goat door that you did not choose
- You are then offered the chance to switch

At first, staying seems just as good as switching. But the key is that your original choice had a probability of only 1/3 of being correct, while the other two doors together had probability 2/3.

When the host opens a goat door from those two, that 2/3 probability does not disappear — it concentrates on the one remaining unopened door.

So:

- Probability your original choice was right: 1/3
- Probability the other unopened door is right: 2/3

That is why switching is better.

A useful way to see this is to imagine 100 doors instead of 3. You pick 1 door, and the host opens 98 goat doors. It becomes much easier to see that your first choice is probably wrong, and that the one remaining unopened door is much more likely to contain the prize.

What makes this problem interesting is not just the answer, but the fact that intuition pushes so strongly in the wrong direction. It is a good example of how mathematical reasoning can correct instinctive thinking.
